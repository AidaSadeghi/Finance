# Finance-FinacialMarkets



In this section, I will outline the essential financial principles you need to understand when analyzing data. To effectively evaluate company data, it is important to have a strong foundation in corporate finance and accounting, as well as the ability to read and interpret financial statements. Additionally, knowledge of capital markets, financial planning and wealth management, risk management, credit analysis, and ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) factors is crucial.

This course presentation on Corporate Finance Fundamentals by the Corporate Finance Institute (CFI) explores the core pillars of business value maximization and the professional landscape of the industry.Core Principles of Corporate FinanceThe primary objective of corporate finance is to increase a company's total value by strategically managing resources while navigating the tradeoff between profitability and risk. This is achieved through three main activities:

**Capital Investments**Identifying and funding projects or business ventures that offer the best returns adjusted for risk.

**Capital Financing**Selecting the most effective mix of debt and equity to fund those investments and refine the company's financial structure.Dividends and Capital Returns: Determining the timing and method for returning wealth to shareholders.1. Capital Investments and ValuationCapital investments are expenditures intended to provide economic benefits for more than one year, such as building new facilities, launching products, or acquiring other companies.

**Valuation Methods**Companies evaluate these opportunities using Net Present Value (NPV)—the current value of all future cash flows—and the Internal Rate of Return (IRR)—the expected annual growth rate the project will generate.Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A): This involves buying or merging with other businesses to achieve growth or cost savings (synergies). The process typically follows a 10-step path from strategy and search to final integration.2. Capital Financing and StructureFinancing involves deciding where the money comes from—typically through a combination of debt (loans) and equity (ownership shares).

**Optimal Capital Structure**Firms strive for a balance that minimizes their Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).The "Capital Stack": Funding is prioritized by seniority. Senior debt (like bank loans) is repaid first and carries lower risk, while equity is repaid last but offers higher potential returns.Underwriting: This is the process where financial institutions raise capital for corporations by selling debt or equity to investors.3. Dividends and Returning CapitalWhen a company has excess cash, management must decide whether to keep it for future growth or return it to owners.

**The Decision Rule** Generally, if the expected return on a new internal project is lower than the company’s cost of capital (WACC), the funds should be returned to investors.Distribution Methods: This is done via Dividends (direct cash payments) or Share Buybacks (repurchasing shares to increase the value of remaining ones).Career Paths in Corporate FinanceThe industry is divided into several primary sectors:Investment Banking ("Sell Side"): Fast-paced, competitive roles focused on sales, capital markets, and client-facing advisory.

**Institutions** ("Buy Side")Roles in private equity or fund management that focus on investing capital to generate returns.Public Accounting: Focused on audit and advisory with a clear, structured career path.Corporate Roles: Internal finance positions within companies, managing the firm's own capital and strategy.
